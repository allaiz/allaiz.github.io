Null Hypothesis products

To Escape Wage Slavery Without Being A Slave Owner, I need to figure out what I want to sell. What is something that people are going to want to buy, that I can supply?

I have found three main types of advice about this while wandering the entrepreneurial Internet over the years. The first is the “small experiments” idea promoted in one form or another by writers such as Cal Newport, and beloved by Venture Capitalists and the loss-making online “Start Up” community. This idea holds that it’s unknowable what products will be a success, so all you can do is try lots of things until something inevitably takes off. Under this model, the most important thing is to keep the cost of trying very very low, so that you can afford the failures and so the successes will more than pay for them.

I think this “small experiments” idea has some merit, especially the part about keeping the cost of failure low. I am more attracted however to a second perspective put forward by people like Bob Moesta, which suggests that actually there are lots of clues as to why products succeed, if you only know how to understand what the needs and motivations of your customers are.

But I am planning to base my product development on a third idea, that is most neatly articulated in a book that I am using as my go-to reference for starting my business: Rework by Jason Fried and David Heinemeir Hanson of Basecamp.

This idea is: When it comes to understanding needs and motivations, I have a much clearer view of my own than anyone else’s. So if I build a product offering to meet my own needs, I am able to do so with a nuanced understanding of what is important, and I will be able to create a much more compelling and valuable product with much less effort and fewer mistakes. In the software development world where I grew up this is a well established principle known as “scratching your own itch”.

I know from friend’s experiences that this still doesn’t guarantee there will be demand for a product; I guess sometimes the thing you want doesn’t exist because actually no one else wants it… but I think creating the product I want to use is probably the best way for me to at least narrow down the options and increase the chances of finding something good.

The particular “itch” that I have right now is how to set up all the mundane record keeping that I need for my company, and that will be needed as I invite other Self Employers to join my Joint Venture. I need Allaiz.com email addresses. I need a register of company documents such as the shareholder’s agreement and articles of incorporation. I need an accounting ledger that I can use to record transactions and prepare tax returns. I need Internet hosting and off-site backups for all of this.

Because I am an experienced Linux sysadmin, I have the option of self-hosting software for all of this myself. If something works for me, then it can also be a product that I can offer to customers.

But.

I get something valuable from self-hosting that my customers would not. I get autonomy and control over the app and over my data, and I because I own the system I can use it as much as I want for free once I have set it up. This means that my app looks more valuable to me than it would to anyone else. I might prefer to run Apache OFBiz rather than subscribe to Xero simply for this reason alone, but from my customer’s perspective I have just created another SaaS app, and if I didn’t have other reasons to prefer OFBiz to to Xero, then why should I expect that they would either?

And it would be hard work. After 25 years in the industry, I’m acutely aware that setting up a self-hosted app, let alone packaging it as a product offering, means lots of study and learning, lots of effort, and an ongoing daily commitment to keeping the @#%$ thing up and running. It needs to be worth it. There needs to be a compelling reason that existing products are not able to meet my needs, so that by building something to address that need I will create something unique and valuable.

This actually pushes me all the way back to running “small experiments”, only running them on myself rather than on customers. It means my Null Hypothesis should be that there is already an existing SaaS product for everything I need to do, and I should experiment by using them until, hopefully inevitably, I find something they can’t do that is frustrating enough that it’s worth building something to do it myself.

In a way I might have already stumbled across one of these; I have been setting up my own apps on a dedicated server at Hetzner because I found that in 2020, the price/performance ratio for hardware has changed enough that all the existing “cloud” alternatives are offering too little benefit and introducing too much pricing risk. I’m not quite sure how I could help other Self Employers through this without it just becoming another overpriced and under-helpful cloud platform, but I’m going to keep thinking about it in case there is something there.
